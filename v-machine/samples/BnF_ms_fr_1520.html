Encoding Principles

An online aggregate edition of Marguerite de Navarre's Heptaméron, bringing together transcriptions of all surviving manuscripts and sixteenth-century printed editions of the text.

All text of both manuscripts and printed editions has been transcribed; for manuscripts, this includes scribal additions, deletions, substitutions, and annotations. Most paratext has also been transcribed, with the exception of running headers.

In instances of obvious spelling or typographical errors not witnessed by other manuscripts in the tradition, both the original text and the corrected version have been transcribed and tagged according to section 3.4 (Simple Editorial Changes) of the TEI Guidelines.

De Thou's spelling and contractions, which reflect sixteenth-century efforts to align orthography more closely with pronunciation, have been respected. However, for the sake of convenience and clarity, the following changes have been made, and have not been tagged.

We have distinguished "i" from "j" and "u" from "v" in keeping with modern usage.

De Thou makes occasional use of the circonflex and the tréma, as well as of the acute accent, primarily on vowels that would commonly be followed by an "s" in Middle French and/or a circonflex in modern French (e.g. "béte" instead of "beste" or "bête"). We have respected this practice, but we have added grave accents to disambiguate homonyms (la/là, a/à, ou/où, etc.) and acute accents to final é/és/ée/ées as needed.

Our transcription does not account for differences in letterform (e.g. "ʃ" versus "s").

De Thou's capitalization is not regular according to modern French usage. Accordingly, capital letters at the beginning of common nouns have been changed to lowercase (except at the beginning of a sentence), and lowercase letters at the beginning of proper nouns have been changed to capitals.

De Thou's scribal abbreviations have been expanded, with both the abbreviation and the expansion tagged.

De Thou uses periods, commas, colons, and interpuncts (‧) to mark pauses, but without regularity. To avoid imposing interpretations on the text through the introduction of punctuation, we have tried to respect De Thou's punctuation to the extent that it is possible to do so, although De Thou's usage often makes it difficult to distinguish periods from interpuncts. As such, we have used periods where De Thou's punctuation mark is followed by a capital letter, or before ampersands (&) where a full stop would occur in modern French. In all other instances, we have used interpuncts. In rare instances where a punctuation mark would be called for in modern French and is not found in De Thou's text, we have introduced it and tagged it as follows: ..

De Thou does use apostrophes to mark contractions and liaisons (e.g. "el'ut" instead of "ele ut"), but not to distinguish contracted articles, particles, or possessives (e.g. "lan" instead of "l'an", "samie" instead of "s'amie", or "nut" instead of "n'ut"). As such, we have introduced untagged apostrophes where appropriate.

De Thou does not use grammatical hyphens (e.g. "sanbletil" instead of "sanble-t-il"). We have respected his usage except in a few rare instances, where, for the sake of hyphenation, we have introduced hyphens in keeping with modern French usage and tagged them as follows: -.

De Thou does use hyphens to indicate that a word continues on the next line. We have represented this usage with pipes (|).

De Thou does not use any kind of quotations marks to denote direct discourse. We have respected this usage.
